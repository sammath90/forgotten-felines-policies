# ADCH Minimum Welfare & Operational Standards

**Source:** Minimum Welfare Operational Standards Booklet Aug 24
**© Association of Dogs and Cats Homes 2025**
**Last Updated:** December 2022 | Latest clarifications to guidance published August 2024

---

ADCH is the major force in raising the standards of animal welfare across the sector and ADCH's Members are strongly supportive of this as being a key aim for the Association. One fundamental aspect of this is these Minimum Standards are approved by Trustees and Members. These form the basis of Membership, and hence of decisions on admission of new Members.

Members and ADCH want their Minimum Standards to:
- Safeguard animal welfare
- Benefit the rescue's reputation
- Improve and maintain good practice in animal welfare
- Assist in preparing for licencing of our sector, and run concurrent to existing legislation in jurisdictions where it is applicable
- Be proportionate and realistically achievable.

Throughout this document, the words **"must"** and **"shall"** indicate a requirement. The word **"should"** indicates a recommendation.

**General guidance notes:**
- Members need to be mindful of complying at all times with any relevant legislation within their own jurisdiction.
- Requirements herein apply only to animals in the legal ownership of the organisation. Members acting as agents on behalf of local authorities may not have legal ownership until the conclusion of the statutory stray day period or equivalent.
- Compliance with these Standards cannot confer immunity from legal obligations.

## Glossary

1. **'Sufficient number of people'** — Numeric ratios are not prescribed. In calculating the people-to-animal ratio, consideration must be foremost given to ensuring sufficient capacity to provide the five welfare needs for every individual animal onsite, including cleaning and emergency/preventative healthcare within prescribed timescales. Sufficient people will take account of all animals and activities undertaken on the entire site.

2. **'People'** — Includes paid staff and volunteers (not the public or adopters).

3. **'Competent'** — Able to demonstrate, through training records and work or volunteer experience, the ability to discern how to apply the five welfare needs to all animals under their care.

4. **'Welfare needs'** — Each individual animal must have its five welfare needs met:
   - The need for a suitable environment
   - The need for a suitable diet
   - The need to be able to exhibit normal behaviour patterns
   - The need to be housed with, or apart from, other animals
   - The need to be protected from pain, suffering, injury and disease.

5. **'Qualification'** — Organisations can check legitimacy and qualification levels on:
   - OFQUAL (England and Northern Ireland): https://register.ofqual.gov.uk/
   - QW (Wales): https://qualificationswales.org/
   - SQCF (Scotland): https://scqf.org.uk/
   - Irish Register of Qualifications: https://irq.ie/

6. **'Veterinary advice'** — Can be over the telephone where appropriate, as well as appointments in a veterinary clinic or a 'vet round' in a centre.

7. **'Virtual'** — Use of video conferencing/calling (e.g. Facetime, WhatsApp, Zoom, Skype), supported by Google Earth for satellite imagery.

8. **'Routinely'** — As part of the organisation's standard operating procedures. Excludes emergency, one-off boarding which could not have been planned for (up to 72 hours).

9. **'Feral'** — Cats that originate from a free-living lifestyle and typically have no or limited interactions with or dependency on humans. Adult feral cats are not suitable to be kept as companion animals. Kittens born from feral adults may be suitable if socialised from 2–7/8 weeks of age.

## Admission and Intake of Animals

### Admission and Veterinary Checks

**Standard 1** — When animals are received, they shall be thoroughly scanned for a microchip using an ISO-compliant scanner. When a microchip, tag or tattoo is found, reasonable attempts shall be made to trace the registered owner in line with section 149 of the Environmental Protection Act 1990. Scanning several times increases the chance of finding a microchip. Organisations should have a procedure for discovering foreign microchips, including interim animal/human health precautions and a protocol of non-endemic disease testing agreed with their veterinary practice.

**Standard 2** — Where no microchip is found, unless against veterinary advice, all domestic animals shall be microchipped and registered with an appropriate UK or Irish database as soon as possible, with the organisation listed as keeper until the point of rehoming, and especially prior to being walked off site or placed on foster.

**Standard 3** — The health and welfare of all animals shall be assessed on intake and monitored at least daily. Animals shall be examined by a veterinary professional or a suitably competent lay person as soon as possible (no more than 72 hours). Animals with perceived health problems must be seen by a veterinary surgeon as soon as possible. Dogs and cats must be examined by a veterinary surgeon whilst in care, unless there is documented evidence of a veterinary check in the last 6 months.

**Standard 4** — Animals shall be vaccinated on or shortly after arrival into the centre under the direction of the veterinary surgeon (unless recent evidence of vaccination or vet/behaviourist advises against).

## Care of Animals

### Food and Water

**Standard 5** — All animals shall be supplied with an appropriate diet, suitable for species, age, life stage and condition. Clean, potable water shall be available at all times and changed daily. Feed and water must be unspoilt and free from contamination.

**Standard 6** — Feed and water intake must be monitored, and any problems recorded and addressed. Animals must not remain without eating or drinking for longer than 24 hours without veterinary advice. Signs of significant weight loss or gain require immediate veterinary support.

**Standard 7** — Cats shall be offered at least two separate meals a day approximately 8 hours apart during the daytime.

**Standard 8** — Where animals are fed together, care shall be taken to ensure all animals receive sufficient food. In communal animal areas, there shall be at least one feeding and water bowl for each animal.

**Standard 9** — Eating vessels shall be cleansed or disposed of after each meal. Drinking vessels shall be cleaned at least once a day.

**Standard 10** — Where feed is prepared on any premises, hygienic facilities must be available including a working surface, hot and cold running water and storage. Human and animal food preparation should not take place in shared areas at the same time or using shared utensils.

### Disease Control and Vaccination

**Standard 11** — Precautions, as advised by the veterinary surgeon, shall be taken to prevent and control the spread of infectious disease and parasites amongst animals, staff, volunteers and visitors.

**Standard 12** — Where an animal shows signs of infectious disease or illness, advice must be sought from a veterinary surgeon and isolation measures or barrier nursing put in place. A documented procedure should be held covering the organisation's plans for infectious and potentially infectious animals.

**Standard 13** — Adequate isolation facilities for animals with infectious diseases shall be available (on site or via formal documented arrangement with a local provider such as a veterinary practice).

**Standard 14** — An animal first aid kit shall be available for use at any animal welfare centre or premises with more than 5 pens. Veterinary contact details should be kept up to date and easily accessible.

### Cleansing and Disinfecting

**Standard 15** — All units, corridors, common areas, kitchens etc. shall be kept clean and free from accumulations of dirt and dust. Moveable items should be removed for cleaning at least weekly. Note: cleaning practices must balance hygiene with animal psychological welfare — removing familiar scents can cause distress.

**Standard 16** — Each occupied unit shall be cleaned daily. All excreta and soiled material shall be removed from all areas used by animals at least daily and more often if necessary.

**Standard 17** — All bedding areas shall be kept clean and dry. Animals shall have access to a dry area whilst their housing is being cleaned. Animals must be removed during high pressure or steam cleaning.

**Standard 18** — Each unit shall be thoroughly cleaned, disinfected and dried at a change of occupancy.

**Standard 19** — Facilities shall be provided for the appropriate storage, treatment and disposal of all classifications of waste including wash down water and 'controlled wastes'.

**Standard 20** — Care shall be taken with cleaning routines to protect animals from disinfectant poisoning or injury (e.g. caustic burns). Phenolic disinfectants are toxic to cats and should not be used near dogs.

**Standard 21** — Chemicals shall be used, handled, and stored strictly in accordance with the manufacturers' instructions.

### Cat Litter Trays

**Standard 22** — In cat units, suitably sited and sized litter trays, which are easy to clean and impermeable, shall be provided at all times. They shall be placed as far away as possible from resting and feeding areas.

**Standard 23** — Litter trays shall be large enough to allow the cat to move around, dig and cover faeces and urine without feeling restricted. A suitable absorbent material shall be provided, deep enough to absorb urine and allow the cat to dig and cover.

**Standard 24** — Litter trays shall be scooped at least once a day. In multiple cat units, one litter tray shall be provided for each cat.

### Environment and Companionship

Animals need to be able to exhibit normal behavioural patterns. Consideration shall be given as to whether it is appropriate to house with or apart from other animals. This applies whether they are in a kennel environment or fostered.

**Standard 25** — All animals shall have the opportunity to exercise daily.

**Standard 26** — All cats and dogs shall have toys and/or feeding enrichment to provide opportunities to perform natural behaviour such as hunting or play.

**Standard 27** — Items provided shall be the correct size and type for the individual animal and its behaviour. Items should be durable, clean, safe, and capable of being easily cleaned and disinfected. They should be checked daily and replaced when necessary.

**Standard 28** — Both cats and dogs shall have human contact and interaction on a daily basis.

**Standard 29** — All puppies and kittens shall be adequately and appropriately socialised and habituated to prevent fear behaviour towards animals, situations, environments and people.

**Standard 30** — Animals shall never be forced to interact with people, and shall have the facility to avoid people should they wish (e.g. a hiding place or a high vertical resting place).

**Standard 31** — Dogs shall be protected from animal company which causes them fear, frustration or distress, but provided with enjoyable animal company where possible.

**Standard 32** — In kennels where dogs are housed separately and in the vicinity of other dogs, each dog shall be able to control their view of other dogs.

**Standard 33** — Dogs shall be introduced to one another slowly and in a positive way.

**Standard 34** — Decisions to pair dogs shall be made by competent persons. They shall not be housed together if either tries to avoid the other or appears fearful.

**Standard 35** — Pregnant, whelping, nursing bitches and puppies under seven weeks of age shall be accommodated somewhere safe and quiet. Bitches with puppies should ideally be placed in a foster home.

**Standard 36** — Puppies shall not be housed away from the bitch or rehomed until they are at least eight weeks of age, unless under veterinary guidance.

**Standard 37** — In addition to toys and feeding enrichment, cats shall be given facilities for scratching. Cats prefer vertical surfaces. Scratching posts should be tall enough for the cat to use fully stretched.

**Standard 38** — Only compatible cats shall be housed together. Decisions to house cats together shall be made by a competent person. Single housing is the best choice for cats unless they have been living together harmoniously previously.

**Standard 39** — Pair-housed or group-housed cats shall have sufficient space and adequate resources to minimise competition and to be able to avoid each other. This includes a separate litter tray, bed and hiding place for each cat.

**Standard 40** — Cats shall not be housed in the same enclosure as other species even if they have come from the same household.

**Standard 41** — Where cats are housed in the vicinity of other cats, each cat shall be able to hide from every other cat. Barriers between cats should be opaque.

**Standard 42** — Kittens shall not be housed away from the queen or rehomed until they are at least eight weeks of age, unless under veterinary guidance.

**Standard 43** — Pregnant, queening or nursing queens and suckling kittens shall be accommodated away from the main cattery units, somewhere safe and quiet, ideally in a foster home or a separate maternity unit.

**Standard 44** — Weaned kittens shall be housed with their littermates.

### Grooming

**Standard 45** — All animals shall be groomed when necessary and steps taken to ensure dogs and cats coats are free of mats.

### Supervision, Including Staff and Volunteer Training

**Standard 46** — Whilst animals are at the establishment there must be a contactable keyholder within reasonable distance to the premises. Emergency plans must be reviewed and updated annually.

**Standard 47** — Animals must not be left unattended, other than in their housing facilities, in any situation or for any period likely to cause them distress (e.g. tethered unattended, in overflow crates, or held in carriers beyond transportation needs).

**Standard 48** — Sufficient numbers of competent people must be available to provide a level of care that ensures all animals' welfare needs are met. In foster homes, the foster carer must be trained and given guidance by the organisation. Training must cover:
- Animal welfare, including recognising poor welfare
- Animal handling
- Animal behaviour
- Cleanliness and hygiene
- Feeding and food preparation
- Disease control
- Recognition and first aid action for sick animals
- New information on disease and inherited disease, legislation and behaviour

The training policy should be reviewed and updated annually. Training records for individual staff and volunteers should be retained.

### Feral Cats

**Standard 49** — Feral (unsocialised, unowned) cats are not suitable to be kept as companion animals and therefore there shall be no attempt to socialise them to humans. No attempt should be made to socialise an adult cat or kitten that is consistently fearful or distressed in the presence of humans.

**Standard 50** — Adult feral cats must not be kept in confinement any longer than necessary (maximum 48 hours unless immediate veterinary intervention is needed). They must be provided with any necessary immediate care, neutered and ear-tipped, and then released into a suitable environment as soon as possible. Ear tipping should enable identification of neutering from a distance to avoid recapture.

### Veterinary Care

**Standard 51** — Each organisation shall nominate one or more veterinary practices or veterinary surgeons who can advise on the health of animals entering and resident within the organisation.

**Standard 52** — All veterinary products shall be stored, used and disposed of as required by the product datasheet and COSHH legislation. Unnecessary access to medicines should be prevented by storing medicines in a lockable location.

## Transportation of Animals

Vehicles used to transport animals shall be roadworthy and regularly serviced. The organisation shall ensure compliance with relevant legislation pertaining to the transport of animals.

**Standard 53 — Transportation of dogs:**
- Dogs are suitably restrained (seat belt harness, dog cage or dog guard)
- Cages are of adequate size, provide good ventilation, and are firmly secured out of direct sunlight and away from heating vents
- The dog must be able to stand, move around and lie down with absorbent material in the carrier
- Not transported when temperature poses risk
- On long journeys: offered food at intervals of no more than 8 hours and water at no more than 4 hours; given opportunity to relieve themselves; not left unattended for unreasonable periods; not left in vehicles where temperature may pose a risk
- Journeys over 6 hours should not be undertaken unless by exception; a 1-hour stop for toileting and water should be taken, then a further 6 hours with an 11-hour rest period before transport recommences
- Pregnant bitches should not be transported unless for veterinary purposes

**Standard 54 — Transportation of cats:**
- Carriers of adequate size, providing good ventilation, firmly secured out of direct sunlight and away from heating vents; cat must be able to stand, move around and lie down
- Not transported when temperature poses risk
- On long journeys: offered food at intervals of no more than 8 hours and water at no more than 4 hours; provided with a litter tray or absorbent material; not left unattended for unreasonable periods
- Journeys over 6 hours should not be undertaken unless by exception
- Cats should always be transported singly unless a mother and her kittens, or a litter under 12 weeks
- Litters under 8 weeks should be transported together; unweaned litters only transported with the mother
- Cats should have their view protected from other cats
- Additional vigilance for cleanliness and risk of infection when transporting feral cats
- All vehicles and equipment must be regularly cleaned and disinfected
- Cats should not be left in a vehicle where temperature may pose a risk

## Assessment of Animals

### Training and Behaviour Modification

**Standard 55** — Each organisation shall be able to seek the assistance of a suitably trained and experienced individual (internally or externally) who can evaluate and recommend appropriate positive behavioural modification programmes for dogs and cats that exhibit unwanted behaviour.

**Standard 56** — All training and behaviour modification shall be done through positive reinforcement methods without the use of coercive or punitive techniques and/or equipment. Slip-leads should not be used as a training aid or for exercise purposes.

### Animal Assessment

**Standard 57** — The characteristics of each particular animal shall be assessed in order to identify the most suitable type of home. Factors to consider include temperament, age, reactions with people and other animals, physical contact and grooming, location of home, exercise opportunities, and experience of the potential adopter.

**Standard 58** — Particular care shall be taken when considering rehoming a dog in a new home with young children.

## Animal Departures

### Home Fostering

**Standard 59** — Where animals are kept in animal housing units by foster carers, these units shall meet the housing conditions specified in Annex 1a for dogs and/or 1b for cats.

**Standard 60** — Each home used to provide foster care shall be visited in person or virtually, assessed and approved by a suitably knowledgeable member of the organisation's staff or volunteers before it is used. This assessment must ensure the home environment is suitable and the foster carer is competent to meet the animals' needs.

**Standard 61** — For dogs, an assessment shall be made and steps put in place to avoid the risk of overcrowding and hoarding, and agreement reached with the foster carer.

**Standard 62** — For cats, an assessment shall be made and steps put in place to avoid the risk of overcrowding and hoarding. Cats from different sources shall not be mixed. Cats shall not be mixed with resident cats or other foster cats. There should be a room to which the cat is confined, which should be easily cleanable.

**Standard 63** — Dogs shall not be fostered in homes where they are likely to be left on their own long enough for them to become distressed.

**Standard 64** — Assurances shall be given that foster homes will make arrangements for dogs to be given exercise sufficient for the size and breed of the dog and to meet its welfare needs.

### Neutering

**Standard 65** — The organisation shall have all cats neutered, unless against veterinary advice. Where it has not been possible to neuter before rehoming, a system for ensuring the animal is neutered shall be put in place.

**Standard 66** — Healthy kittens shall be neutered before they become sexually mature (by 4 months) where the organisation has access to a vet carrying these out, and shall be neutered before rehoming wherever possible.

### Euthanasia

Every animal shall have a life worth living. Euthanasia can be a response to circumstances in which health and/or welfare are irreversibly and severely compromised and when it is not possible to meet an animal's needs.

**Standard 67** — Any decision to euthanise an animal shall be made on a case-by-case basis, taking into account the individual animal. In most cases, except for feral cats, this decision shall only be made after rehabilitation has been explored and a full and thorough examination carried out. A consultation should take place with the individual or individuals who have full knowledge of the animal.

**Standard 68** — The key factors in deciding whether euthanasia shall be considered are:
- (a) To prevent pain or suffering (including mental and emotional)
- (b) If an animal poses an unacceptable risk to people or to other animals
- (c) If quality of life for the animal is compromised to an unacceptable degree, with no realistic prospect of improvement
- (d) Any other good reason under the organisation's operational procedures and/or its euthanasia policy

**Standard 69** — Euthanasia shall only be carried out by or under the direction of a veterinary surgeon using an approved humane method. Intravenous injection is the recommended method.

**Standard 70** — Each organisation shall have a written policy on euthanasia.

### Rehoming

**Standard 71** — Information about the prospective new owner/keeper and their family shall be obtained to ensure their circumstances and facilities fit the requirements of the particular animal. Proof of address shall always be obtained.

**Standard 72** — Prospective adopters and any dogs they have shall meet the dog they are considering adopting. Cats shall also meet their prospective adopters. This meeting should be under supervision.

**Standard 73** — The prospective new owner shall be given specific advice and relevant details about their particular new pet covering its future care, diet, behavioural and welfare needs, veterinary history, and ongoing requirements.

**Standard 74** — On being adopted, every animal shall:
- Have been vaccinated and treated for parasites
- Have a matt-free coat and be groomed if appropriate
- Have been fully examined before departure
- Have a record of health, breed, believed age, and vaccinations given to the new owner
- Have any previous or ongoing health issues disclosed to the new owner

**Standard 75** — It shall be made clear to the new owner that they are welcome to contact the organisation for advice. The organisation should assist in returning any animal at the request of the new owner.

## Legal and Administrative

### Management of the Rescue

**Standard 76** — The organisation shall keep records for all animals for which it is responsible, including description, date of arrival and departure, believed age, vet history, behavioural assessments, name and address of the new owner/keeper, and a record of what details were passed to the new owner.

**Standard 77** — Personal data should be held in accordance with GDPR and the organisation's own data protection and retention policies.

**Standard 78** — The organisation should have written procedures, followed by all responsible for animal care and available to all staff and volunteers, for:
- Feeding
- Cleaning
- Transportation
- Prevention and control of disease spread
- Animal monitoring
- Death of an animal
- Escape of an animal (on and off premises)
- In an emergency

A written emergency plan must be in place if animals are kept in premises, known and available to everyone, covering fire, flooding, breakdowns of essential systems, and other emergencies.

**Standard 79** — Where members of the public can view or come into contact with animals, suitable measures must be taken for the safety and welfare of the animals including staff supervision, signage and locked doors.

**Standard 80** — Where organisations use third-party facilities (such as commercial boarding) to routinely house their animals, they must physically inspect the premises prior to the agreement and when self-assessments are requested. A formal record of the inspection must be kept.

## Annexes A and B — Housing Conditions

### A) Dogs Housing Conditions

- **A1** — Sleeping areas shall be sufficiently insulated and/or heated to prevent extremes of temperature.
- **A2** — Fences shall be secure and safe with appropriate spacing to prevent escape or entrapment.
- **A3** — All dog kennels shall be secure to prevent escape.
- **A4** — Ventilation shall be provided to all interior areas of dog kennels.
- **A5** — Dog units shall have a sleeping area.
- **A6** — All dogs shall be provided with a bedding area or bench large enough to allow the dog to lie comfortably. Space shall allow the dog to stand up, sit fully erect, turn around and lie down in a natural position.
- **A7** — Suitable bedding shall be provided, capable of being easily cleaned and disinfected. Bedding shall be sited out of draughts and maintained clean, parasite-free and dry.
- **A8** — Dog exercise areas shall not be used as overnight sleeping areas.
- **A9** — Heating facilities shall be available in the unit and used according to requirements of the individual dog.
- **A10** — Sleeping accommodation shall be maintained at ambient temperature appropriate for species and breed. Minimum 10°C in part of the sleeping area; maximum normally 26°C.
- **A11** — Heaters shall not present risk of burning, electrocution, or fire. Open flame appliances shall not be used.
- **A12** — Dogs shall always have the option to move to a cooler, shady area in hot weather.
- **A13** — All dog beds, sleeping platforms and bedding shall be durable, safe, capable of being easily cleaned and disinfected.
- **A14** — Where raised beds are provided, they shall be appropriate to the age of the dog and materials shall not be toxic to dogs.
- **A15** — The type of bedding used shall be safe, soft and easily cleaned or disposed of after use.
- **A16** — Crating shall not be used to house dogs permanently or as a long-term alternative to suitable kennel accommodation.
- **A17** — Crates shall be large enough to allow the dog to stand, turn around, sit erect and lie down. The dog shall have regular opportunities to toilet and shall not be left inside for more than four hours at a time.

### B) Cats Housing Conditions

- **B1** — The cattery shall be structurally sound, constructed of robust and safe materials and be well maintained.
- **B2** — Full length and width sneeze barriers shall be provided where the gap between cat units is less than 62.5cm.
- **B3** — Waste water shall not run off into adjacent cat pens.
- **B4** — There shall be an escape-proof area (safety corridor/entrance lobby) at the exit of each cat unit.
- **B5** — There shall be a safe, secure, waterproof roof over all cat units and the safety corridor. Exercise area materials shall be capable of filtering UV light and providing adequate shade.
- **B6** — There shall be adequate lighting in the cat unit.
- **B7** — Ventilation shall be appropriate all year round (cool in hot weather, avoiding cold draughts in winter). Localised draughts in sleeping accommodation shall be avoided.
- **B8** — Cats shall always have the option to move to a cooler, shady area in hot weather.
- **B9** — Cats need separate sleeping accommodation providing somewhere to hide. All cats shall be provided with a bedding area or bench large enough to allow the animal to lie comfortably. Sleeping area height should be between 75cm and 1.5m.
  - 1 cat: 0.85 sq. m
  - 2 cats: 1.1 sq. m
  - Up to 4 cats: 1.7 sq. m
- **B10** — Shelves or raised areas shall be provided to allow cats to rest high up.
- **B11** — Heating facilities shall be available in the unit and used according to requirements of the individual cat.
- **B12** — Cat sleeping accommodation shall be maintained at ambient temperature appropriate for species. Normally no less than 10°C, with part of the sleeping area at least 15°C; maximum normally 26°C.
- **B13** — Cats shall be able to remove themselves from any additional source of heat.
- **B14** — Heaters shall not present risk of burning, electrocution or fire. Open flame appliances shall not be used.
- **B15** — Suitable bedding shall be provided which allows the cat to be comfortable and which is capable of being easily and adequately cleaned and disinfected.
- **B16** — A cat shall have space for safe play and exercise.
  - 1 cat: 1.65 m²
  - 2 cats: 2.2 m²
  - Up to 4 cats: 2.8 m²
  - Minimum height: 1.8m
- **B17** — Cats shall be provided with a place to hide which shall be large enough to provide concealment (e.g. cardboard boxes, igloo type beds, curtain or blanket).

## Annex C — Importation of Dogs and Cats from Outside the UK and Ireland

The following standards relate to transfers outside of the UK, Republic of Ireland, Channel Islands and Isle of Man.

- **C1** — Members and applicants shall notify ADCH if they plan to import dogs and/or cats from outside the UK, Ireland, Channel Islands and Isle of Man.
- **C2** — Dog and cat importation from within the EU shall be carried out following the Balai Directive (Directive 92/65/EEC).
- **C3** — Members importing from outside the EU shall seek additional advice from their vet and ADCH and ensure legal compliance.
- **C4** — Any imported dog or cat shall have a documented behavioural assessment carried out by a suitably competent person in the country of origin, showing suitability for rehoming in a domestic environment.
- **C5** — Dogs and cats shall only be imported after veterinary tests show the animal is disease free. Key diseases of concern: Rabies, Babesiosis, Ehrlichiosis, Leishmaniasis and Dirofilaria.
- **C6** — Blood tests shall be done using an EU Approved Laboratory (e.g. Acarus laboratory at Langford Vets).
- **C7** — If free from exotic disease, animals shall be given appropriate preventative veterinary treatment.
- **C8** — Upon arrival, the animal shall move directly into the care of the organisation and ADCH Standards shall apply.
- **C9** — Organisations shall have a plan in place in case the rehoming of an animal is not successful.

**Important considerations for importation:**
- Long distance transportation is very stressful for cats and dogs
- Animals may not have been socialised/habituated to life in a UK/Irish home
- Exotic diseases may be imported, increasing disease risks to the native population
- International rehoming is seen as a temporary solution
